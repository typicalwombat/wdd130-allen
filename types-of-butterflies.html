Types of Butterflies

Butterflies are classified into 7 different families. Each family has unique characteristics and beautiful species.

1. Nymphalidae - The Brush-Footed Butterflies

The largest butterfly family Nymphalidae butterflies are known for their reduced front legs, which are covered in tiny hairs and look like brushes—hence the name brush-footed. Their wings are often bold, bright, and patterned. Common Members: Monarchs, Viceroys, Painted Ladies, Admirals, Fritillaries Key Features: -Front pair of legs too small for walking -Strong, fast fliers -Wide variety of colors and patterns -Frequently seen in gardens and fields

Fun Fact: Monarch butterflies migrate thousands of miles, making one of the longest insect migrations in the world.

2. Pieridae – The Whites and Sulphurs

Light-colored butterflies that love sunny open spaces Pieridae butterflies are usually white, yellow, or pale orange. They’re common in meadows, yards, and open fields. Common Members: Cabbage White, Clouded Sulphur, Orange Sulphur Key Features: -Mostly white or yellow wings -Dark dots or tips along the wings -Quick, fluttering flight patterns -Often attracted to flowering plants

Fun Fact: The Cabbage White is one of the most widespread butterflies in the world and is often the first species new butterfly watchers recognize.

3. Lycaenidae – The Blues, Coppers, and Hairstreaks

Small but brilliantly colored butterflies This family includes some of the smallest butterflies—and some of the brightest. Their wings often sparkle in blues, purples, or metallic tones. Common Members: Spring Azure, Eastern Tailed-Blue, American Copper, Hairstreaks Key Features: -Generally small (about the size of a quarter) -Many species have iridescent wings -Caterpillars often have mutualistic relationships with ants -Delicate, fast wingbeats

Fun Fact: Hairstreaks sometimes have tiny “false heads” on their wings to confuse predators.

4. Papilionidae – The Swallowtails

Elegant butterflies with long tail-like extensions Swallowtails are large, graceful butterflies recognized by the “tails” on their hindwings. They are often brightly colored and prefer warm habitats. Common Members: Eastern Tiger Swallowtail, Black Swallowtail, Giant Swallowtail Key Features: -Large wingspan and striking colors -Tail-like extensions on hindwings -Caterpillars have an organ called an osmeterium that emits a smell to deter predators -Found in gardens, woodlands, and meadows

Fun Fact: The Giant Swallowtail is the largest butterfly in North America.

5. Riodinidae – The Metalmarks

Tiny butterflies with shiny, metallic markings Metalmarks are named for the metallic-looking spots on their wings. They are diverse but less commonly seen compared to other families. Common Members: Northern Metalmark, Mexican Metalmark Key Features: -Small and delicate -Wings often contain silver or bronze “metallic” spots -Prefer warm, sunny habitats -Sit with wings open more often than closed

Fun Fact: Some species mimic the wing patterns of toxic butterflies to avoid predators.

6. Hesperiidae – The Skippers

Fast, darting butterflies that look a bit like moths Skippers get their name from their quick, skipping flight. They have thicker bodies and hooked antennae, which make them look different from other butterflies. Common Members: Silver-Spotted Skipper, Fiery Skipper, Sachem Key Features: -Thick, fuzzy bodies -Hooked or curved antenna tips -Rapid, zigzag flight -Usually brown, tan, orange, or gold

Fun Fact: Although skippers look moth-like, they are true butterflies and are active during the day.

How to tell them apart.

Here’s a quick guide to spotting differences: Family Size Wing Colors Behavior Special Traits Nymphalidae Medium–large Bold patterns Strong flyers Brush-like front legs Pieridae Medium White/yellow Fluttering Wing-tip dots Lycaenidae Small Metallic blues/coppers Fast wingbeats Ant partnerships Papilionidae Large Bright, elegant Slow gliding Tail-like hindwings Riodinidae Small Metallic spots Sun-loving Shiny wing patches Hesperiidae Small–medium Browns & oranges Skipping flight Hooked antennae
